When Nothing Feels Certain,
What Do You Hold On To?

By Randel Yumul

photo from: https://www.pinterest.com/pin/30751209944926262/

Lately, it feels like we're all living in the in-between—waiting for answers, unsure of what's next. Maybe you're waiting on test results, wondering about your future, or just waking up every day feeling like the world is a little less stable than it used to be. If that sounds familiar, you're not alone.

A recent study by researchers Kienzler and colleagues (2024) describes how we've moved past what they called the “golden age of predictability.” In its place? A world that feels heavier, faster, and more uncertain. And that uncertainty doesn't just sit quietly in the background—it seeps in, stirring up anxiety, heightening depression, and leaving us floating without something solid to hold on to.

Psychologist Kate Sweeny from UC Riverside has spent years studying this uncomfortable space—that limbo of not knowing. Her research has followed people through those tense, quiet moments: students awaiting bar results, patients in diagnostic limbo, even all of us during lockdowns. One consistent thread emerges from her work: the waiting, the not-knowing, the silence—it's all exhausting. Time stretches. Fear grows. And clarity feels just out of reach.

“I Don't Like Mirrors” by Alev Neto

Uncertainty is rarely loud—it doesn't announce itself. Instead, it settles quietly, like fog. It blurs the outlines of things you used to see clearly: plans, routines, even parts of yourself. Some days, it feels like you're walking through a thick mist, unable to see more than a step ahead. You try to act normal, go about your day, answer messages, get groceries—but underneath, something tightens. A quiet hum of worry that won't turn off.

But in the midst of that restlessness, it helps to become absorbed in something, anything, that allows the noise to quiet down. That could mean painting, writing, tending a garden, or getting lost in a rhythm—activities that fully draw your attention and, in doing so, let time slip by more gently. It's not about distraction, but immersion. Those small pockets of deep focus offer a kind of refuge, a space where the weight of waiting feels a little lighter.

photo from: https://www.pinterest.com/pin/30751209944926262/

Then there's mindfulness—not the lofty kind, but the simple awareness of now. Feeling the heat of your tea. Watching the rain. Noticing your feet on the floor. In a world full of “what ifs,” staying present—just for a moment—can offer surprising relief.

And perhaps most of all, communication matters. Sweeny's research reminds us that what hurts isn't always the outcome, but the lack of clarity. When we're waiting, vague timelines only deepen fear. Something as simple as setting an expectation—“We'll call by Friday”—can make the weight feel lighter.

Waiting may always feel like a storm cloud hovering nearby, but there's power in naming that feeling and grounding yourself anyway. You can soften the edges of uncertainty with small acts of self-compassion. Maybe it's turning off your notifications for a while, or saying no to another task that drains you. Maybe it's journaling through the waiting, not to solve anything, but to witness your own experience.

If nothing else, hold on to what helps you wait well. A slow walk. A favorite playlist. A friend who doesn't ask you to explain. You may not be able to change the uncertainty, but you can give yourself softness, structure, and the space to feel what you feel.

Uncertainty doesn't vanish overnight. It lingers like a shadow. But it doesn't have to swallow you whole either.
