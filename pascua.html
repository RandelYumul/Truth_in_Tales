Burnout Is the New Broken Wing:
Why We're Still Expected to Fly

By Maria Niña Grace Pascua

“The Lament for Icarus"

-Herbert James Draper

When people talk about burnout, they often treat it as a personal failure. You couldn't handle the pressure. You couldn't keep up. But maybe the problem isn't that we flew too close to the sun — but that the world kept telling us to fly higher. Like Icarus, many of us weren't reckless, we were just trying to be free.

In the myth, Icarus didn't fall because he was weak, he fell because he soared. Given a chance to rise above the prison he was born into, he took it. He didn't ignore the warnings out of pride, he just wanted to feel the sun on his face. His father, Daedalus, urged caution: “Don't go too low, don't go too high, stay in the middle to be safe.”

But Icarus chose exhilaration, purpose, and flight. And that choice —brief, brilliant— cost him everything. Today, we're still expected to fly. Students must always excel, workers must always be available, caregivers must never complain. Rest is mistaken for laziness. Fatigue is a weakness to hide. “The incentive to hustle is a defense mechanism to make ends meet and exceed high expectations” (Balkeran, 2020). You're praised for pushing past your limits — until you break.

The consequences aren't just emotional — they are deadly. According to the World Health Organization (2021), over 398,000 people died in 2016 due to health problems caused by overworking. Burnout isn't just a buzzword; it's costing lives. But how can you know better in a world that teaches you to keep going no matter what? Exams stacked on sleepless weeks. Jobs that expect devotion without pause. Like wax wings held together by ambition, we are built to fly—just not for long.

“Landscape with the Fall of Icarus”

-Pieter Bruegel de Oude

And even when we fall, the world continues. In William Carlos Williams' 'Landscape with the Fall of Icarus' poem inspired by Pieter Bruegel the Elder's painting, the myth unfolds not in center stage, but almost as an afterthought. You have to search the canvas to spot Icarus' legs, barely visible as he disappears into the sea. Meanwhile, the foreground is alive with activity: a farmer plows his field, a shepherd watches his sheep, ships sail on, and life goes on.

The contrast is striking, a dramatic fall met with utter indifference. When we break down, the world doesn't stop to notice. Our suffering, however personal and consuming, becomes a symbol, not just of myth, but of how burnout feels: invisible, unnoticed, and swallowed by routine.

Burnout isn't a weakness. It's not a lack of discipline. It's a consequence of a system that rewards overreach and punishes rest. Just like Icarus wasn't weak for wanting more, we are not weak for collapsing under the weight of impossible expectations. The tragedy isn't that we broke, it's that we were expected to never break at all.

“Landscape with the Fall of Icarus”

-Pieter Bruegel de Oude

The story of Icarus is often told as a warning. But maybe it should be retold as a question: Why are we building wings we know can't last?

He laughed as he fell—not out of foolishness, but of understanding. The joy was in the flight. But not everyone should have to crash just to prove they can rise.

To have flown shouldn't mean to have broken.

Published July 21, 2025
